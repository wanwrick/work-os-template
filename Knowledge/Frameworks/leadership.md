## 1. Managing & Leading Organizations

### Congruence Model (Nadler-Tushman)
Diagnose organizational performance by examining fit between four components:

```
        Environment → Strategy →
                                 ┌──────────────┐
                    Inputs →     │    Work       │
                                 │    People     │  → Outputs
                    Resources →  │    Structure  │    (Org, Group, Individual)
                                 │    Culture    │
                                 └──────────────┘
```

**Key insight:** Performance problems arise from MISFIT between components, not from any single component being "wrong."

### After Action Review (AAR)
Post-event learning protocol:
1. **What was supposed to happen?** (Intent, plan, objectives)
2. **What actually happened?** (Facts, timeline, observations)
3. **Why was there a difference?** (Root causes, systemic factors)
4. **What will we do differently?** (Specific actions, owners, timelines)

Rules: No rank in the room. Facts before opinions. Blame-free. Document and share.

### Leadership Choices Framework
Every leader makes choices across these dimensions:
- **Tasks vs. People:** What to prioritize in each situation
- **Directing vs. Empowering:** How much autonomy to give
- **Short-term vs. Long-term:** Which horizon to optimize for
- **Individual vs. Team:** When to decide alone vs. collectively
- **Stability vs. Change:** When to hold course vs. adapt

### Intellectual Humility
- Recognize the limits of your own knowledge
- Actively seek disconfirming evidence
- Separate ego from ideas; change your mind when evidence warrants
- Create psychological safety for others to challenge you

### Emotional Intelligence (Goleman)
1. **Self-Awareness:** Recognize your own emotions and their effects
2. **Self-Regulation:** Control impulses, think before acting
3. **Motivation:** Internal drive beyond money and status
4. **Empathy:** Understand others' emotional states
5. **Social Skills:** Build relationships, influence, collaborate

### Accountability Framework
```
Clarity → Capability → Commitment → Consequences
```
- Is the expectation clear?
- Does the person have the skills and resources?
- Have they genuinely committed?
- Are there meaningful consequences (positive and negative)?

## 2. Transformational Leadership

### Full-Range Leadership Model
Three leadership styles on a continuum:

**Laissez-Faire (Avoid):** Absent leadership. No engagement, no direction. Worst outcomes.

**Transactional Leadership (Manage):**
- **Contingent Reward:** Clear exchanges: "If you do X, you get Y"
- **Management by Exception (Active):** Monitor for deviations, correct
- **Management by Exception (Passive):** Intervene only when problems arise

**Transformational Leadership (Inspire):**
The Four I's:
1. **Idealized Influence:** Be a role model. Walk the talk. Earn trust through consistency.
2. **Inspirational Motivation:** Articulate compelling vision. Create meaning. Set high expectations.
3. **Intellectual Stimulation:** Challenge assumptions. Encourage innovation. Reframe problems.
4. **Individualized Consideration:** Coach, mentor, recognize unique needs. Develop people.

### Charismatic Leadership Tactics (CLTs)
12 tactics for increasing perceived charisma:
- Metaphors, stories, anecdotes
- Contrasts and comparisons
- Rhetorical questions
- Three-part lists
- Expressions of moral conviction
- Reflections of group sentiments
- Setting high goals
- Conveying confidence
- Animated voice and gestures
- Facial expressions
- Body gestures
- Symbolic signals

### Ethical Leadership
- **Character:** Integrity, honesty, trustworthiness
- **Competence:** Ability to lead effectively
- **Courage:** Willingness to make difficult ethical decisions
- **Humility:** Recognition of one's own limitations

Test: Would you be comfortable if your decision was on the front page of the newspaper? Would you explain it to your family without shame?

## 3. Leadership Teams

### Lencioni's Five Dysfunctions of a Team
```
         Results
        /       \
    Accountability
      /           \
    Commitment
    /             \
  Conflict
  /               \
Trust (Foundation)
```

1. **Absence of Trust** → Invulnerability. Fix: vulnerability-based trust exercises
2. **Fear of Conflict** → Artificial harmony. Fix: mine for productive disagreement
3. **Lack of Commitment** → Ambiguity. Fix: clarity and closure after debate
4. **Avoidance of Accountability** → Low standards. Fix: peer-to-peer accountability
5. **Inattention to Results** → Status and ego. Fix: collective results focus

### DiSC Assessment Framework
| Style | Priorities | Strengths | Under Stress |
|-------|-----------|-----------|-------------|
| **D (Dominance)** | Results, action, challenge | Decisive, direct, competitive | Impatient, insensitive |
| **i (Influence)** | Enthusiasm, collaboration, action | Persuasive, optimistic, social | Disorganized, impulsive |
| **S (Steadiness)** | Support, stability, collaboration | Patient, reliable, team player | Resistant to change, passive |
| **C (Conscientiousness)** | Accuracy, stability, challenge | Analytical, careful, systematic | Overly critical, isolated |

### Social Contract for Teams
Explicit agreement on how the team will operate:
- How we communicate (channels, response times, escalation)
- How we make decisions (consensus, vote, leader decides)
- How we handle conflict (direct conversation first, mediator if needed)
- How we give feedback (specific, timely, behavior-focused)
- How we hold each other accountable (public commitments, check-ins)

### Psychological Safety (Edmondson)
The belief that you won't be punished for speaking up with ideas, questions, concerns, or mistakes.

Build it by:
- Frame work as a learning problem, not an execution problem
- Acknowledge your own fallibility
- Model curiosity: ask questions
- Create structures for input (round-robins, anonymous input)
- Respond productively to bad news

### 360-Degree Feedback
- Collect from: Manager, peers, direct reports, self
- Focus on behaviors, not personality
- Look for patterns, not individual ratings
- Use for development, not evaluation
- Follow up with action plan and accountability partner

## 4. Effective Presentations

### Monroe's Motivated Sequence
Persuasive speech structure:
1. **Attention:** Hook the audience (story, statistic, question)
2. **Need:** Establish the problem (make them feel it)
3. **Satisfaction:** Present your solution
4. **Visualization:** Paint the future (with and without solution)
5. **Action:** Clear call to action

### RAMP Openings
Four ways to hook an audience:
- **R**hetorical Question: "What if I told you...?"
- **A**nalogy: "This is like..."
- **M**etaphor: "Our data platform is the nervous system of the organization"
- **P**ersonal Story: Brief, relevant, emotional connection

### SUCCESs Principles (Heath & Heath)
Make ideas stick:
- **S**imple: Find the core message
- **U**nexpected: Break patterns, create curiosity gaps
- **C**oncrete: Use specific examples, not abstractions
- **C**redible: Use data, testimonials, demonstrations
- **E**motional: Make them feel something
- **S**tories: Wrap ideas in narrative

### STAR Moments
Signature moments that audiences remember:
- **S**omething **T**hey'll **A**lways **R**emember
- One dramatic moment per presentation
- Could be: striking visual, live demo, personal revelation, audience participation

### Executive Presence
- **Power of Pause:** 2-4 seconds after key points
- **Speak Third:** Not first (eager) or last (caution); third (considered)
- **Language Upgrades:** "I think" → "The data suggests" | "Maybe" → "I recommend"
- **Physical Presence:** Grounded stance, purposeful gestures, eye contact
- **Q&A Handling:** Pause → Rephrase → Answer → Bridge back to key message

### PREP Framework (Impromptu Speaking)
- **P**oint: State your main point
- **R**eason: Explain why
- **E**xample: Give a specific example
- **P**oint: Restate your point

### BLUF (Bottom Line Up Front)
Lead with the conclusion, then provide supporting details. Essential for executive audiences.

## 5. Critical Thinking

### System 1 vs. System 2 Thinking (Kahneman)
- **System 1:** Fast, automatic, intuitive, emotional. Prone to biases.
- **System 2:** Slow, deliberate, analytical, effortful. More accurate but resource-intensive.

Use System 1 for routine decisions. Engage System 2 for high-stakes, novel, or complex decisions.

### 10-10-10 Decision Framework
Before deciding, ask:
- How will I feel about this in **10 minutes**?
- How will I feel about this in **10 months**?
- How will I feel about this in **10 years**?

Helps overcome short-term emotional reactions and consider long-term implications.

### Know / Assume / Don't Know Analysis
For any decision:
1. **What do I KNOW?** (Facts, evidence, data)
2. **What am I ASSUMING?** (Beliefs, estimates, guesses)
3. **What DON'T I KNOW?** (Gaps, unknowns, risks)

Test assumptions. Fill knowledge gaps. Make the implicit explicit.

### IDEALS Problem-Solving Method
- **I**dentify the problem
- **D**efine the context
- **E**numerate choices
- **A**nalyze options
- **L**ist reasons pro and con
- **S**elf-correct (check for biases)

### Common Logical Fallacies
- **Ad Hominem:** Attacking the person, not the argument
- **Straw Man:** Misrepresenting someone's argument to defeat it
- **False Dilemma:** Presenting only two options when more exist
- **Appeal to Authority:** Using authority instead of evidence
- **Confirmation Bias:** Seeking only evidence that supports your view
- **Sunk Cost Fallacy:** Continuing because of past investment
- **Survivorship Bias:** Drawing conclusions only from winners
- **Anchoring Bias:** Over-relying on first piece of information
