## 1. Business Strategy

### Porter's Five Forces
Determines industry attractiveness and profitability potential.

| Force | Key Questions | High = Bad for Incumbents |
|-------|--------------|---------------------------|
| **Rivalry** | How intense is competition? | Many equal competitors, slow growth, high fixed costs |
| **Buyer Power** | Can customers push prices down? | Few buyers, undifferentiated product, switching ease |
| **Supplier Power** | Can suppliers push costs up? | Few suppliers, unique inputs, switching costs |
| **New Entrants** | How easy to enter this market? | Low barriers, weak brands, low capital needs |
| **Substitutes** | Are alternatives available? | Close substitutes, low switching costs |

### VRIN/VRIO Framework
Does the firm have sustainable competitive advantage?

| Criterion | Question | If No → |
|-----------|----------|---------|
| **Valuable** | Does it exploit opportunity or neutralize threat? | Competitive disadvantage |
| **Rare** | Do few competitors possess it? | Competitive parity |
| **Inimitable** | Is it costly to imitate? | Temporary advantage |
| **Non-substitutable / Organized** | Can the firm exploit it? | Sustainable advantage |

Sources of inimitability: Path dependence, causal ambiguity, social complexity, legal protection.

### Value Chain Analysis
```
Primary Activities: Inbound Logistics → Operations → Outbound Logistics → Marketing & Sales → Service
Support Activities: Firm Infrastructure, HR, Technology Development, Procurement
```
Identify where value is created and where costs accumulate. Focus resources on activities that create differentiation or cost advantage.

### Generic Strategies (Porter)
- **Cost Leadership:** Lowest cost producer (scale, efficiency, tight cost controls)
- **Differentiation:** Unique product/service commanding premium (brand, quality, innovation)
- **Focus:** Narrow segment with cost or differentiation advantage
- **Stuck in the Middle:** Trying both = inferior performance (Porter's warning)

### Platform Economics & Network Effects
- **Direct Network Effects:** More users → more valuable for each user (phone, social media)
- **Indirect Network Effects:** More users on one side → more valuable for other side (marketplace)
- **Platform Strategy:** Build multi-sided platform, subsidize one side, monetize the other
- **Winner-take-all dynamics:** Network effects + switching costs + data advantages
- **Envelopment:** Platform extends into adjacent market using existing user base

### Game Theory in Strategy
- **Nash Equilibrium:** No player can improve by unilaterally changing strategy
- **Prisoner's Dilemma:** Individual rationality leads to collective suboptimality
- **Credible Commitment:** Make it costly to reverse your strategy
- **Signaling:** Actions that reveal private information (capacity expansion, pricing)
- **First-Mover vs. Fast-Follower:** Trade-off between market creation and learning from others' mistakes

## 2. Role of the General Manager

### Strategic Planning Process
```
Environmental Analysis → Strategy Formulation → Strategy Implementation → Evaluation & Control
         ↑                                                                        │
         └────────────────────── Feedback Loop ──────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Environmental Analysis Tools

**PESTLE Analysis:**
- **P**olitical: Government policy, regulation, stability
- **E**conomic: Growth, inflation, exchange rates, interest rates
- **S**ocial: Demographics, culture, trends, education
- **T**echnological: Innovation rate, R&D, automation, digital
- **L**egal: Employment law, consumer protection, antitrust
- **E**nvironmental: Climate, sustainability, resource scarcity

**Industry Analysis:** Porter's Five Forces (see above)

**Internal Analysis:** VRIN/VRIO + Value Chain

### Blue Ocean Strategy
| Red Ocean | Blue Ocean |
|-----------|------------|
| Compete in existing market | Create uncontested market space |
| Beat the competition | Make competition irrelevant |
| Exploit existing demand | Create and capture new demand |
| Value-cost trade-off | Break the value-cost trade-off |

**Four Actions Framework:**
- **Eliminate:** Which factors can be eliminated?
- **Reduce:** Which factors can be reduced below industry standard?
- **Raise:** Which factors can be raised above industry standard?
- **Create:** Which factors can be created that the industry never offered?

### Discovery-Driven Planning (McGrath & MacMillan)
For ventures with high uncertainty -- plan backwards from desired outcome.

1. Define the required profit (reverse income statement)
2. Determine the milestones and checkpoints
3. Identify the assumptions (most to least critical)
4. Plan to test assumptions at each checkpoint
5. Kill or pivot if key assumptions fail

### Stakeholder Analysis
| Stakeholder | Interest | Power | Strategy |
|-------------|----------|-------|----------|
| High Power, High Interest | Key players | High | Manage closely |
| High Power, Low Interest | Keep satisfied | High | Keep satisfied |
| Low Power, High Interest | Keep informed | Low | Keep informed |
| Low Power, Low Interest | Minimal effort | Low | Monitor |

### Change Management (Kotter's 8 Steps)
1. Create urgency
2. Form a guiding coalition
3. Create a vision for change
4. Communicate the vision
5. Remove obstacles
6. Create short-term wins
7. Build on the change
8. Anchor changes in culture

## 3. Global Strategy

### CAGE Distance Framework
Predicts difficulty of doing business between countries.

| Dimension | Factors | Impact |
|-----------|---------|--------|
| **Cultural** | Language, religion, values, norms | Trust, communication, marketing |
| **Administrative** | Legal system, political ties, corruption | Compliance, risk, governance |
| **Geographic** | Physical distance, borders, climate | Logistics, communication, travel |
| **Economic** | Income levels, development, resources | Market size, cost structures, demand |

**Example:** US expanding to Japan scores high on Cultural (language, business norms) and Geographic distance, but low on Administrative (stable legal system) and Economic (similar income levels). Contrast with US to Canada: low distance on all four dimensions.

### AAA Strategies for Global Expansion
- **Adaptation:** Modify offerings for local markets (localization)
- **Aggregation:** Create regional/global economies of scale (standardization)
- **Arbitrage:** Exploit differences between markets (labor, tax, regulation)

Most firms emphasize one or two. Trying all three creates complexity.

### Entry Mode Selection
```
Risk/Control Spectrum:
Export → Licensing → Franchising → Joint Venture → Wholly-Owned Subsidiary

Low risk/control ←──────────────────────────→ High risk/control
```

**International Joint Venture (IJV) Considerations:**
- Partner selection (complementary resources, compatible cultures)
- Governance structure (equity split, board composition, decision rights)
- Knowledge protection (what to share, what to protect)
- Exit provisions (buyout clauses, dispute resolution)

### Hofstede Cultural Dimensions
- **Power Distance:** Acceptance of unequal power distribution
- **Individualism vs. Collectivism:** Group vs. individual orientation
- **Masculinity vs. Femininity:** Competition vs. cooperation values
- **Uncertainty Avoidance:** Tolerance for ambiguity
- **Long-term vs. Short-term Orientation:** Future planning vs. present focus
- **Indulgence vs. Restraint:** Gratification norms

## 4. New Venture Management

### Sahlman's Business Plan Framework
| Component | Key Questions |
|-----------|--------------|
| **People** | Who is on the team? What are their skills, track record, gaps? |
| **Opportunity** | What is the market? How big? How fast growing? What pain solved? |
| **Context** | What macro/industry factors affect this? Regulatory? Competitive? |
| **Risk & Reward** | What can go wrong? What's the upside? How to mitigate risks? |

### First Level Screen
Quick viability check before deep analysis:
- Is there a real pain point?
- Is the market large enough?
- Can you reach customers efficiently?
- Is the business model viable?
- Does the team have relevant expertise?
- Is the timing right?

### Disciplined Entrepreneurship Canvas (DEC)
24 steps organized into 6 themes:
1. Who is your customer?
2. What can you do for your customer?
3. How does your customer acquire your product?
4. How do you make money?
5. How do you design & build your product?
6. How do you scale your business?

### Lean Startup / Hypothesis-Driven Entrepreneurship
```
Build → Measure → Learn → (Pivot or Persevere)
```
- Start with hypotheses, not business plan
- Minimum Viable Product (MVP) to test key assumptions
- Validated learning over vanity metrics
- Pivot types: zoom-in, zoom-out, customer segment, customer need, platform, business architecture

### Market Sizing (TAM/SAM/SOM)
```
TAM (Total Addressable Market): Total market demand if 100% share
SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market): Segment you can reach
SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market): Realistic near-term capture
```
Use both top-down (industry data) and bottom-up (unit economics × target customers) approaches.

## 5. Management Simulation

### Strategy Diamond (Hambrick & Fredrickson)
Five interconnected elements of strategy:

1. **Arenas:** Where will we be active?
2. **Vehicles:** How will we get there?
3. **Differentiators:** How will we win?
4. **Staging:** What will be our speed and sequence?
5. **Economic Logic:** How will we earn returns?

All five must be internally consistent. Changing one often requires adjusting others.

### Simulation Learning Principles
- **Pricing Strategy:** Balance market share vs. margins; watch elasticity
- **Capacity Planning:** Build ahead of demand but avoid idle capacity costs
- **EPS Growth:** Market rewards consistent earnings growth; avoid volatility
- **Financial Optimization:** Manage debt/equity mix for optimal WACC
- **Competitive Dynamics:** Watch competitor moves; don't start price wars you can't win

## 6. Consulting Frameworks

### Structured Problem Solving
```
1. Define the problem (hypothesis)
2. Break into components (issue tree / MECE)
3. Prioritize (80/20 rule)
4. Analyze each component
5. Synthesize findings
6. Recommend (pyramid principle)
```

### MECE (Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive)
- Every element fits in exactly one category
- No gaps: all possibilities covered
- Foundation for issue trees and structured thinking

### Business Capability Mapping
Map the organization's capabilities to identify strengths, gaps, and investment priorities. Capabilities are what the business does (not how), organized hierarchically.

### Domain-Driven Design
- Align technical architecture with business domains
- Bounded contexts define clear boundaries
- Ubiquitous language shared by business and tech
- Critical for data platform architecture
